## Fresh approach: phased /lowy + /hickey

Everything below this section is the *as-built* record — the production failure,
the hard constraints, the salvage of <PrLink pr={1034} />. This section is the
plan now being *executed* — **A1 (<PrLink pr={1055} />) and A2 (<PrLink pr={1063} />)
have landed; B — the daemon, the one-way door — is all that remains.** It began
as the counterfactual: **if we discarded that PR and built R-4 afresh knowing all
of it**, how would we decompose and phase it? It is the output of a fresh `/lowy`
(Lowy / Parnas volatility-based decomposition) and `/hickey` (*Simple Made Easy*)
pass over the *whole* feature — every user-facing behaviour, the correctness
semantics, and the four production failure modes — taking nothing about the
shipped decomposition for granted.

<Callout kind="note" title="The thesis">
Decompose by *concept*, not by package or process-lifecycle; rank the concepts
*most-stable → most-volatile* (the axes below). But the *build* order is set by a
different fact: the process decouple is the only **one-way door** — the single
step that changes runtime topology (IPC, a surviving process, single-instance,
reattach), so the only one that's genuinely risky and hard to bisect. Everything
is either *before* the door or *part of* it. So the work collapses into a low-risk
in-process foundation — **A1** (the structural refactor) then **A2** (the
build/commit identity + the staleness-key hash, isolated for its own review) —
whose only job is to shrink the door, and the door itself (**B**), shipped with
its *entire* safety and honest-state surface.

**What's allowed to be a separate phase is decided by hazard, not by module or
convenience.** The door *creates* the hazards — a dead daemon (the empty-canvas
lie), a wire-incompatible one (a bricked server), a live restart that can lose the
session (#1034), an outdated daemon the user can neither see nor act on.
Everything that defuses or honestly surfaces those hazards is hazard-coupled to
the door and ships *with* it; nothing in that surface may drift to a follow-up —
that drift *is* the bug #1034 paid for. Only what is neither hazardous nor
meaningless-without-the-door can live before it, and that is exactly the
in-process foundation. The one move that still matters most: identity/staleness is
designed *in* as **A2**, before any daemon exists — a typed, build-enforced
wire+behaviour closure — so a wire change can't escape the staleness key by
construction; currency is then a read-site derivation (`staleKey !== currentBuildId()`),
never a stored boolean. **Lowy supplies the volatility ranking; Hickey supplies
the within-unit shape** ("restart" and "status" are compositions and single-owner
projections, not god-functions).
</Callout>

These eight axes collapse into **three build units**, split by the one-way door
(the process decouple). Before the door, in-process: the structural foundation —
PTY ownership, the contract, and the DAG seam (**A1**) — and the build/commit
identity + staleness key, carved out for its own review (**A2**). The door itself
— connection liveness, currency, recovery, the kill/exit invariant, and the
health→UI projection — is **B**.

### Three units: the foundation (A1 · A2), and the door (B)

No feature flags — each unit ships complete to `master` and leaves Kolu working.
The in-process foundation comes first, as two separately-reviewable PRs: **A1**
(the structural refactor — extract, contract, serve-in-process, DAG) and **A2**
(the build/commit identity + the staleness-key hash). Their only job is to make
**B** — the one-way decouple — as small as a topology change can be. **B** then
ships the decouple *together with its entire safety and honest-state surface*,
because shipping it without the canvas / forced-restart / recovery / chip
reintroduces the failures the postmortem below catalogues. The boundary rule is
**asymmetric**: *hazard-coupled* work must stay together (so B is one PR despite
its size, and nothing in it may drift to a follow-up), but *hazard-free* work may
be split however helps review — which is exactly why A1/A2 is a clean cut (both are
safe and in-process; neither gates the other's safety). Each unit names its **LOC
estimate** (grounded in the analogous code on this branch; excludes one-time
relocation), its **risk of breaking Kolu**, and its **user-visible change**.

the pre-restart session is intact on disk, status is `dead`, the error rethrows.
`kill -TERM` kolu-server leaves the daemon alive in its own cgroup; a second daemon
against a held pid-file `exit(0)`s. The `daemonStatus` payload contains *only*
`{state}`; a hypothetical `reconnecting` state touches one module.
</Callout>

*Three PRs: A1, A2, B.* A1 and A2 are split purely for review clarity — both are
safe and in-process, and neither gates the other (the asymmetric rule: hazard-free
work splits freely). B stays *one* PR despite its size — hazard, not convenience —
built as ordered commits (topology → reattach → recovery → honest-state UI) that
land together because no part of its safety surface may ship without the rest. Work
genuinely beyond R-4 — the ssh transport (R-2), mid-session reconnect resilience
(R-3), session export/import (already shipped, <PrLink pr={1046} />) — builds on B
afterward.

### What this makes impossible by construction

The four production failures stop being review-time vigilance and become a *failing
unit test at the phase that owns the concept*:

| Production failure | Lens diagnosis | Killed at | How |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Mis-scoped staleness key (a wire change didn't flip it) | Lowy: boundary leaked across packaging · Hickey: concept inferred from a directory accident | **A2** | The survivor's closure *is* the package; the staleKey hashes its source, and a build-time guard fails if the contract reaches a wire/behaviour module outside that package. The mis-scope can't recur. |
| Over-prompting (whole-binary hash nudged every deploy) | Lowy: keyed on the wrong volatility axis | **A2** | The closure-scoped key flips iff a restart gains terminal-host code; a server-only change leaving the key unchanged is a falsifiable test, not a tuning regret. |
| Data-loss restart (kill-then-pray) | Lowy: un-encapsulated sequence-volatility · Hickey: a 7-concern god-function | **B** | The recovery mechanism — one composed restart shared by the forced + user paths — ships *with* the door, so "kill before snapshot" is structurally impossible and two destructive code paths collapse to one. |
| Empty-canvas lie (dead daemon → "you have no terminals") | Lowy: failure presentation had no owning boundary · Hickey: place-over-value | **B** | Honest liveness as a value and a single owned health→UI projection both ship with the door, so the canvas cannot get it wrong — the honest state is part of B, never a follow-up. |

Three further structural braids dissolve with them: currency-stored-not-derived (a
frozen `outdated` on the connection) → a read-site derivation in **B**;
identity-with-no-typed-receptacle (four loose strings, an inlined `!==`, a
copy-pasted `BuildRef`) → one `PtyHostIdentity` defined in **A2**, rendered through
one shared `srv · pty` rail (both columns + the server proxy in **A2**, the
divergence semantics in **B**); and the health cell over-streaming four diagnostic
ids to every browser → a **B** cell that carries only `{state}`, provenance via a
one-shot `buildInfo`.

### Where Lowy and Hickey disagreed

- **Health cell vs provenance (in B).** Lowy would tolerate keeping the identity pair *on* the status cell (one wider value vs an extra round-trip); Hickey insists on splitting (different concepts, change-rates, consumers). *Resolved:* split, but provenance is a *one-shot* `buildInfo` RPC into a signal — not a streaming cell — so the extra wire surface is a single connect-time call, satisfying Lowy's own "keep the cell minimal" warning too.
- **Recovery as one mechanism (in B).** Lowy wants the restart *sequence* encapsulated once; Hickey warns that forcing the forced-restart (nothing to preserve, PTYs already doomed) and the user-restart (session must survive) through one body risks complecting two failure semantics — easiness masquerading as simplicity. *Resolved:* one composed mechanism whose step-values *parameterise* what-to-preserve (`captureSession` returns an empty context on the forced path) — one ordering owner, no tangled body.
- **Identity shape.** Both agree the key-vs-commit *value* split is correct; the residual tension is encoding + storage. *Resolved:* `PtyHostIdentity` is the single emitted value (Lowy's receptacle); currency is *derived* from its `staleKey` at the read site (Hickey's derivation); the browser receives only the readout projection, so identity-for-staleness never leaves the server.
- **Live currency's new transition.** A read-site derivation introduces a `connected→outdated` mid-session flip the frozen design never had. *Resolved:* B scopes `currentBuildId` as memoised / process-stable and adds the two-snapshot test; a genuinely runtime-changing key is deferred to R-3 with its own UI-transition test, not silently assumed.

### The risk profile

Two risk levels: **A1** and **A2** are <Pill variant="ok">low</Pill> —
byte-identical or additive, user-invisible, the full feature suite as their net;
**B** is <Pill variant="bad">high</Pill> — the one-way door. There is no middle.
The whole point of A1 and A2 is to be the cheap, confidence-building work you land
first so that B, when it comes, is the *only* variable in play and arrives against
an unchanged-behaviour codebase that already speaks the contract. B is an
irreducible spike — with no flags, moving PTYs into a surviving process is one
atomic step — and it carries its entire safety + honest-state surface, because
every part of that surface is hazard-coupled to the door (a dead / incompatible /
outdated / restart-loss state the door itself creates). You shrink everything
*around* the door; you do not pretend the door is small, and you do not let its
safety surface drift out.

This split is derived from the *end-state architecture*, not from how the work
actually shipped. For reference, the incident-driven path that was taken — extract
→ parameterise → daemonise → re-cut the UI after a production failure — is the
as-built record below; its defining mistake was letting the door's safety surface
(honest state + recovery) drift into a follow-up, which is the seam
[#1034](#-production-failure-2026-05-30--the-r4c-ui-restart-that-lost-a-session)
fell through. Here that surface is, *by the hazard rule*, part of B; and
identity/staleness is a first-class boundary (A2), build-time enforced, so the
mis-scoped-key and over-prompting failures are unit tests on day one rather than
late retrofits.

<Callout kind="note" title="Net">
Three units, no feature flags, split by the one-way door. The in-process
foundation — **A1** (extract the primitive · define + serve the contract
in-process · decouple the DAG) and **A2** (the build/commit identity + the
staleness-key hash, carved out for focused review) — is low-risk, user-invisible,
and exists only to shrink the door. **B — the decouple** ships the daemon
*complete*: topology, reattach, currency, the full recovery mechanism (including the
#1034 fix), and the entire honest-state UI — because every piece is hazard-coupled
to the door and none may drift out. The boundary rule: *what's allowed to be a
separate phase is decided by hazard, not by module or convenience.* The deepest
things already proven right — reattach-by-id collapsing two identities into one, the
zero-provider survivor, `getDaemonHandle` refusing a dead client, the metadata
write-fence partition — carry over verbatim.
</Callout>

## ⚠ Production failure (2026-05-30) — the R4c-UI restart that lost a session

The first R4c-UI build (the build-mismatch "update pending" nudge + a *Restart local
PTY daemon* command — <PrLink pr={1034} />) shipped to production (Linux/systemd).
The nudge fired correctly on a deploy; clicking restart then **destroyed a live
20-terminal session and could not bring the daemon back**. macOS was unaffected.
Timeline from the user-service journal:

```
08:26  terminal created, total:20            ← 20 live terminals (Claude Code agents)
08:29  "restarting local PTY-host daemon (user-triggered)"
08:29  "killing all terminals" count:20       ← killAll drains ALL 20 first
08:29  daemon socket closed / old daemon SIGTERM
08:29  (old daemon: 13.5h old, 25G RAM / 40.6G swap — box thrashing)
08:29  "Daemon failed to start: socket did not appear within 30s"  ← RESPAWN TIMED OUT
08:31  new daemon finally binds (~2 min after SIGTERM)
```

<Callout kind="danger" title="Net effect">
killAll ran *before* the new daemon was confirmed up; the respawn timed out at 30s
while the overloaded box took ~2 min to release the old daemon's pid-file + socket.
The user was left with **zero terminals and a dead daemon handle**, and the canvas
rendered **empty** — indistinguishable from "you have no terminals." The 20 PTYs
(running agents + scrollback) were gone; restore re-created only fresh empty shells.
No second daemon ever ran — single-instance held (pid-file `O_EXCL` + liveness
probe, plus the systemd unit name); the failure was the respawn losing the race
against the slow old-daemon exit.
</Callout>

### Hard constraints for the redo

1. **Restart must be recoverable, never kill-then-pray.** `restartLocalPtyHostDaemon` drained all 20 terminals before confirming the new daemon could bind; the respawn then failed with no rollback. A daemon restart inherently loses the PTYs (accepted, confirmed cost) — but the *failure mode* must not be silent loss. Snapshot the session first; after respawn, auto-reattach / offer restore; if respawn fails, surface a loud, recoverable degraded state with the session preserved — never an empty canvas.
2. **Wait for the old daemon to fully exit — the single-instance lock fights the restart.** SIGTERM-then-immediately-respawn races the pid-file (`O_EXCL` + liveness probe) and the `--unit=kolu-pty-host` name; the new daemon can't acquire until the old one is gone. `restartDaemon` waited 5s for the socket and polled 30s for the respawn — both far too short. Wait on the actual process exit (pid gone via `kill(pid,0)`) before spawning, with a generous, load-aware ceiling (prefer event-driven waits over fixed polls).
3. **Timeouts must fit a loaded production box, not an idle dev one.** 20 heavy PTYs tearing down + a tsx cold-start under swap pressure >> 30s. macOS passed only because it was fast. Every restart / spawn deadline needs real headroom.
4. **Never lie about state — show "Connecting…/degraded", not an empty canvas.** A dead/absent daemon handle (`getDaemonHandle()` throwing) rendered as silent emptiness. It must surface as an explicit connecting / degraded UI state. The empty canvas is what made the user believe the session was permanently lost.
5. **A persistent PTY status indicator — always visible.** The only daemon signal today is the amber nudge, shown *only* when `outdated`. There must always be a PTY status chip (distinct from the WebSocket dot) with explicit states — **connected · connecting · degraded/dead · update-pending** — so the daemon's health is always glanceable, exactly like the WS connection dot. *(Being implemented onto #1034 as a first cut.)*
6. **Key staleness on pty-host, not the whole binary.** `currentBuildId()` keys on the `/nix/store/<hash>-kolu-stamped` path = the *entire* kolu build, so *every* deploy (even server/client-only, pty-host untouched) fires the nudge — and acting on it costs a terminal-losing restart for *zero* benefit. Hash the `@kolu/pty-host` source/closure (or bake a pty-host-specific rev) so the soft nudge only fires when a restart actually gains something. Wire-incompatible contract changes stay on the forced-restart path. *(This trade-off was not surfaced at planning — it should have been.)*
7. **Back up the session before a destructive restart.** The saved session survived here only by luck (6 terminals on disk). Snapshot it to a backup before any kill-all / restart so a botched restart is recoverable. *(Done — <PrLink pr={1046} /> shipped Export/Import session commands (`sessionTransfer.ts` in `packages/client/src/` — a ⌘K palette feature): a user-triggered backup/restore hatch for the `SavedSession` blob. The in-restart snapshot/auto-restore also landed with constraints #1–3. Issue <Issue n={1040} /> closed.)*
8. **Fix the preferences storm.** During the churn the client emitted 200+ `rightPanel` `size`/`codeTabTreeSize` patches (bursts ~100–230/min) — a ResizeObserver-style feedback loop that saturated the server and contended with the autosave the restart relies on. Debounce / guard it (pre-existing, not introduced by #1034). *(Filed as <Issue n={1041} />.)*

### Execution postmortem — mistakes made building this PR (don't repeat them)

The [Hard constraints](#hard-constraints-for-the-redo) above are about the *product*
behaviour to build. This section is a different thing: a **postmortem of the build
process itself** — every mistake the salvage of <PrLink pr={1034} /> made and had
to correct mid-flight (production scares, broken builds, duplicate artifacts, git
mishaps). If you are re-cutting this PR, treat each as a pre-committed rule. Grouped
by kind.

#### Architecture / scoping

1. **The staleness hash must cover the daemon's *entire* closure, not just `packages/pty-host/`.** `KOLU_PTY_HOST_BUILD_ID` hashes only `packages/pty-host/**`, but the surviving daemon's wire contract (`ptyHostSurface.ts` + `PTY_HOST_CONTRACT_VERSION`), its entrypoint (`agent/main.ts`), and its startup utils (`daemonUtils.ts`, `buildId.ts`) all live under `packages/server/src/` — *outside* the hash. So an Axis-2 wire-shape change does **not** flip `outdated`; it's caught only if you also remember to bump the contract version. Mis-scoped key = the exact silent-skew bug #6 was meant to kill. **Fix = encapsulation, not a wider fileset glob:** move the whole daemon closure *into* `@kolu/pty-host` so "the survivor" *is* the package and "hash the package" is correct by construction. A glob re-introduces drift the next time a daemon file lands under a new path. Verification test: "if I edit the wire schema, does the build id change?" — if no, the key is wrong. *(Found in prod review; **fixed** in this PR — the closure now lives in `@kolu/pty-host`, bar the thin `--stdio` dispatch shim in `index.ts` which carries no wire shape and stays outside by design; see the [follow-on work](#production-verification-2026-05-30).)*
2. **Two staleness axes — both must be independently detectable.** *Axis 1, build drift* (pty-host source changed, wire still compatible) → soft "update pending" nudge, user restarts when convenient. *Axis 2, wire-incompatible* (schema shape changed) → forced restart at connect (output-validation throw → `IncompatibleDaemonError`), terminals lost. A schema change with *no* contract-version bump must still be caught — which only works once rule 1 is fixed (the hash covers the contract). Don't rely on humans remembering the bump.
3. **Additive wire fields are `optional` + no contract bump.** A required new field makes a surviving (older) daemon's `system.version` fail the new server's schema validation → forced restart → terminals lost, *just to add a diagnostic*. `commitHash` was added optional for exactly this reason: the live prod daemon kept running; its `pty` readout simply showed the non-linked fallback until next restart. Reserve breaking (required) changes — and the `PTY_HOST_CONTRACT_VERSION` bump — for shapes that genuinely can't interoperate.
4. **Identity surfaced to the user must be the navigable one.** The build-id readout first shipped the pty-host *source hash* (`b72d6da`) — not a git SHA, so it wasn't clickable to a commit. Bake `KOLU_COMMIT_HASH` (server + daemon) and link *that*; keep the source hash as the staleness key only. Don't conflate "the staleness key" with "the thing a human clicks".

#### Process / git discipline

1. **Typecheck every package *before* commit/push — never after.** Two commits (<Commit sha="3fd7ea63" />, <Commit sha="b21404ab" />) shipped a broken server build because `Edit`s had *silently failed* (file-changed-since-read) and the missing symbols weren't caught until a later sweep. After any edit, re-grep that the change actually landed, then run `tsc --noEmit` across `common` + `server` + `client` before committing. `nix build` + `tsc` tolerate import cycles that full CI (`just check`'s Biome `noImportCycles`) rejects — so a clean `nix build` is *not* proof CI passes.
2. **Never `git stash` in this worktree.** A `stash`/`switch`/`stash pop` dance to check whether a lint error pre-existed popped an *unrelated* stash into the tree and produced phantom merge conflicts (`CanvasMinimap.tsx`, `Dock.tsx`) that had nothing to do with the work. To check history, use `git show <ref>:<path>` or a throwaway `git worktree`, not stash.
3. **Resolve *all* conflict markers before committing a merge.** The `origin/master` merge (#1046) was committed with unresolved `<<<<<<<` markers still in `commands.tsx` because one resolving `Edit` had failed on a string mismatch — which broke `nix build` in production with "Unexpected token". After resolving, `git grep -nE '^(<<<<<<<|=======|>>>>>>>)'` must return nothing before the merge commit.
4. **Confirm an `Edit` actually applied.** Several edits silently no-op'd because the file had changed since the last read; the duplicate-line and missing-symbol fallout cost extra commits. Re-read or grep after editing; don't assume success.

#### Issue / artifact hygiene

1. **Don't double-create GitHub issues on a flaky tool.** A buffering shell made `gh issue create` look like it failed; retries created *six* issues for two topics (#1040–#1045). Verify with `gh issue list` before retrying; if dupes happen, close the extras pointing at the kept one. *(Cleaned up: kept #1040/#1041, closed #1042–#1045.)*
2. **Never defer with a someday-issue.** Every gap goes into *this* plan and is done in this PR after explicit approval — not filed and forgotten. (Exceptions are genuinely-separate, pre-existing work: the preferences storm #1041, which predates this PR.) Issues filed mid-flight as "follow-ups" (#1040 session-backup, #1047 build-id readout) were in fact pulled back into the PR and completed; the issues were closed, not left open.
3. **Don't trust your own tool transcript over ground truth.** A glitching shell made a file look corrupted (`index.ts` appeared to start with a stray `git diff` line) and made committed work look uncommitted. When the terminal contradicts itself, confirm via an independent path (`node -e` read, a sub-agent, `git show`) before acting — do *not* "fix" a phantom.

## The design (forward)

The long-lived survivor is **`@kolu/pty-host` only** — node-pty fds + the
`@xterm/headless` mirror + the raw VT taps (cwd · title+fgPid · command-run · exit)
+ a unix socket. Everything volatile — the provider DAG (git context, agent
detection, PR status, the most-edited code in the repo) — runs **fresh in
kolu-server**, consuming the daemon's taps over the socket. That split is the whole
point: a kolu-server restart (every deploy) re-runs the providers against the
*surviving* PTYs, so detection is never stale, while the PTYs themselves persist.
Only a change to `pty-host`'s own (rarely-changing) wire contract forces terminal
loss; a provider change (frequent) restarts the cheap in-server layer with PTYs
untouched. This corrects <PrLink pr={1031} />, which daemonized a survivor that
*held* the providers and therefore served stale detection on every deploy.

### Surviving a deploy — the cgroup mechanism <Pill variant="ok">spike-verified</Pill>

On Linux/systemd, kolu-server spawns the daemon via
`systemd-run --user --unit=kolu-pty-host` (gated on `INVOCATION_ID`), landing it in
its **own transient cgroup** — so `systemctl --user restart kolu` (a deploy) does
*not* kill it. A plain detached / `setsid` child does **not** survive on cgroup-v2:
the default `KillMode=control-group` walks *cgroup membership*, not the session, so
the child stays in kolu-server's cgroup and dies with it — the #1031 Linux failure.
On macOS the detached spawn already survives launchd; off-systemd (dev) it's a plain
detached child. Single-instance is guaranteed two independent ways: the systemd unit
name, plus a pid-file (`O_EXCL` create + liveness probe) the daemon acquires on
start.

- **Two prototypes seeded the design:** <PrLink pr={994} /> (the R-2 *remote* prototype) proved "providers in the agent", re-provisioned fresh per version; <PrLink pr={1010} /> was a *local* PTY-only daemon — the thin-survivor model.
